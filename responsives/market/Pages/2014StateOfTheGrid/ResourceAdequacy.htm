Resource Adequacy

 

A problem with reliably operating the grid is occurring; it’s called overgeneration. For well over a year, the ISO has shared the results of its analysis that show as more renewables interconnect to the grid, the more frequently we are generating more power than what can be used; this is what the duck curve shows.

Given the needs of the grid as well as the unyielding law of physics, it is not just a simple matter of turning power plants on and off, or ramp them up and down to different output levels. We are now seeing overgeneration conditions occurring in the middle of the day when renewable solar and wind plants are generating at maximum output and heaping excess on top of generation from natural gas and other traditional plants. The traditional plants must keep operating, for instance, to meet morning ramps before solar plants reach top production levels or fill in generation gaps left when weather conditions prevent renewable plants from operating, such as when clouds pass over a solar panel facility. We can instruct traditional plants to ramp down to their minimum operating level, but this often is not enough; we have to turn off plants. Yet, much of our older generation fleet takes hours, if not days to synch up before producing power, which means they have to be on overnight to handle load in the early morning hours, so turning them off is not an option. Neither is turning off nuclear units, or hydro plants especially during the spring melt. Other units have contracts that prohibit or limit turning them off. Meanwhile, renewable solar and wind plants can’t scale back — they are either on or off. And if we have to turn renewables off, then California will not be able to meet its clean energy goals established by the renewables portfolio standard.

So, the duck chart is more like the canary in the mine; it is showing us that as each year goes by, the mid-day overgeneration situation worsens, which undermines grid reliability as well as driving up costs. The ISO is working with the California Public Utilities Commission, stakeholders and utilities to find a solution, which includes incenting plants owners and developers to deploy fast starting and ramping generation technologies.
